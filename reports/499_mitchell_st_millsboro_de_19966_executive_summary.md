## The Opportunity

This site's value is not the parcel itself — it's the **Indian River Power Plant**, a retired 785 MW coal facility owned by NRG Energy, sitting 0.5 miles away. Indian River has 230 kV and 138 kV transmission interconnection, a 1,200-acre industrial-zoned site, and existing switchyard infrastructure built to handle power flows approaching the scale of our 500 MW target. There are no competing data center projects anywhere in Sussex County, and the New Castle County pipeline (~2.5 GW) is bogged down in permitting fights.

Critically, **US Wind's 1,710 MW offshore wind project** is building three onshore substations at the same Indian River POI. Rather than a conflict, this is a **co-development opportunity**: generation and load at the same substation are complementary, not competing. A 500 MW data center co-located with 1.7 GW of wind generation reduces net grid export, simplifies PJM interconnection studies, provides a guaranteed baseload off-taker for intermittent wind, and creates a compelling narrative for regulators and the community. The recommended path is to approach US Wind directly as a co-development partner.

## PROS

**Existing high-voltage interconnection at scale**
The Indian River substation was engineered for ~785 MW of generation throughput. The switchyard, transformers, 230 kV and 138 kV lines, and transmission rights-of-way are physically in place. This is the single most important asset — it de-risks what is typically the longest lead-time and highest-cost item in a greenfield DC build.

**No competing DC projects in Sussex County**
All five known Delaware data center developers (~2,455 MW total) are targeting New Castle County. No interconnection queue congestion at Indian River. A developer here would have a clear lane to Delmarva Power and PJM without fighting for grid capacity against Starwood's 1.2 GW Project Washington or the Middletown/Newark proposals.

**NRG site potentially available**
The Indian River plant is fully retired (last unit shut down Feb 2025). NRG owns 1,200 acres of industrial-zoned land with no disclosed next use. This is a brownfield opportunity with infrastructure already in place — a potential acquisition, lease, or JV target.

**PJM investing in eastern transmission**
The 2025 RTEP Window 1 includes $11.6B in projects with the Delmarva zone specifically called out. Eastern PJM is getting 500 kV upgrades and a new 765 kV line. While these are 2030+ timelines, they improve long-term deliverability to the Delmarva Peninsula.

**Favorable physical conditions**
Industrial zoning confirmed. Low seismic risk (SDC A). EPA attainment zone — standard air permitting for backup generators. Moderate cooling degree days (1,117). Estimated land cost ~$8M for 150 acres.

## QUALIFICATIONS

**US Wind co-development opportunity — with federal risk on their side**
US Wind's 1,710 MW Maryland Offshore Wind project is building three onshore substations at the Indian River POI (parcel 233-2.00-2.01). Generation and load are complementary at the same substation — a 500 MW DC would consume wind output locally, reducing net grid export by 500 MW, lowering curtailment risk, and simplifying both parties' PJM interconnection studies. A corporate PPA structure would give US Wind a guaranteed baseload off-taker and the DC developer favorable wind rates. The co-development narrative also plays well with the PSC and community ("clean power + local jobs").

However, US Wind faces significant federal headwinds. Trump's BOEM is seeking to vacate the COP approval; the court denied the voluntary remand in Dec 2025, but the admin appealed in Feb 2026. Locally, Sussex County is appealing the substation permit to the DE Supreme Court (though state law SB 159 and the Chancery ruling favor US Wind). Three scenarios:

- *Wind project proceeds:* Best case for co-development. DC gets shared substation infrastructure, PJM queue access, and a long-term wind PPA. 500 MW load co-located with 1.7 GW generation is a strong grid story.
- *Wind project is delayed:* MarWin COD has slipped from 2025 to 2029; Momentum Wind to 2030+. DC developer could still partner on substation buildout and secure interim grid access. Having a capitalized load customer at the POI keeps the Indian River investment alive for US Wind.
- *Wind project dies:* Indian River POI is freed up entirely. NRG's 1,200-acre site and existing switchyard become available. DC developer pursues standalone interconnection with no competing queue positions.

**Statewide interconnection pause blocks near-term action**
PSC Docket 25-0826 has frozen all new large-load (≥25 MW) connections in Delmarva territory. Delmarva's final tariff filing is due April 27. Hearing examiner ruling expected summer 2026. Full commission decision late 2026. No 500 MW facility can enter the interconnection queue until this lifts.

**Large-load tariff terms are undefined**
The tariff will set the economics for any DC in Delaware. Key unknowns: MW threshold (25 vs 50 MW), grid upgrade cost allocation (developer-funded vs. socialized), upfront financial commitments, and default penalties. Until this is resolved, project economics cannot be modeled with confidence.

**No current utility IRP**
Delmarva's last IRP was filed in 2016. There is no public load forecast incorporating the ~2.5 GW DC pipeline or any Sussex County-specific demand projections. This makes it harder to assess how the utility plans to serve incremental load and what transmission investments it's willing to support.

**Sussex County political climate**
The county fought hard against the US Wind substation (4-1 denial, now appealing to the Supreme Court). Community sentiment toward another large industrial facility on or near the Indian River site may be mixed, especially given the Justice40 disadvantaged community designation and the area's history with coal.

## RED FLAGS

**No fiber infrastructure within 30 km**
This is unusual for a DC site and represents a real buildout cost. A new fiber route would need to be constructed — likely north along US-113 toward Dover or the I-95 corridor. Cost and timeline TBD but this is a material capital item and potential schedule risk.

**No water facilities within 30 km**
Air-cooled design would mitigate this, but at 500 MW that adds meaningful cost and footprint. Alternative: source from the Indian River (tidal), but permitting and treatment requirements would be significant.

**No gas pipelines within 50 km**
Nearest gas transmission is ~2 miles from Indian River on US-113 (~$10M+ extension). Limits on-site generation options to diesel or all-electric design.

**No interstate highway access within 30 km**
US-113 is the primary corridor. Not a dealbreaker but affects construction logistics and ongoing operations staffing.

**Environmental Justice designation**
Census tract 10005050602 is a federally designated Justice40 disadvantaged community (diesel PM 76th percentile, housing burden 74th percentile). Additional EJ analysis and community engagement will be required. Given the coal plant history, this could be a sensitive issue.

**Flood risk**
FEMA data was unavailable for the specific parcel, but the adjacent Indian River plant site has significant flood exposure — highest elevation ~30 ft ASL, projected ~4 flood events/year by mid-century.

**Delmarva rate case adds cost uncertainty**
Docket 25-1555: Delmarva is seeking a $67.8M distribution rate increase (21.7%). This sets the baseline that any large-load tariff layers on top of. Combined with the new tariff, all-in electricity costs are unclear.

## BOTTOM LINE

This site is not a slam dunk, but it's substantially better than the automated tool scored it. The Indian River interconnection is a rare asset — and the US Wind co-development angle turns what initially looked like a competing claim into the site's strongest selling point. A 500 MW load paired with 1.7 GW of wind at the same POI is a clean grid story that works in every scenario.

**Recommended next steps:**

1. **Approach US Wind (Nancy Sopko, VP) about co-development** — they have the PJM queue position, the substation permit, and a project under federal threat that a committed load partner would de-risk. Renewable Redevelopment LLC holds parcel 233-2.00-2.01.
2. **Engage NRG Energy** on the broader 1,200-acre Indian River site for campus siting, as a parallel or complementary conversation
3. **Monitor Docket 25-0826** through the April 27 tariff filing and summer hearing — tariff terms set the economics
4. **Initiate PJM pre-application study** for 500 MW load at Indian River to confirm deliverability alongside wind generation
5. **Scope fiber and water buildout** costs early — these are the hardest infrastructure gaps to close

The site's viability hinges on the Indian River interconnection. Without it, this is a 2.6/5 with too many infrastructure gaps. With it — and especially with US Wind as a co-development partner — this is a 3.4/5 with a credible path to 500 MW and potentially the least contested large-load site in Delaware.
